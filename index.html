Water

Imagine that you get up in the morning with the sweet sound of rain against the windows. You look out and the world sings to you. Water makes it possible. Crystal clear water is available right in the comfort of your home. Shortage is not even in the back of your mind because you know that the process of getting that water to you is in the hands of responsible persons. Some forecasts show that by 2025 more than 3 billion people will face water scarcity, but this is not because the world lacks water. The world water crisis is a crisis of management – not one of scarcity. At the global scale, there is enough water to provide “water security” for all, but only if we change the way we manage and develop it. The scarcity of water is a very relative concept that can only be seriously addressed by looking at a basket of factors, including socioeconomic, technical and institutional aspects of water use.
Water is important to our national economies, our leisure and even to our culture. The sustainable management of water resources is of central importance to poverty alleviation, people’s health and the protection of the environment. The best initiatives are those that make a political commitment to consider water as an integral part of sustainable human development, and those which strive to adapt management of the resources to the needs of the poor. Our culture and lifestyles are key factors in the inequitable distribution of water. Changes in attitude and actions will bring about a positive difference, even when those attitudes and actions are as simple as realizing the value of water and remembering conservation practices. Our water use must be through sustainable practices with the aim of safeguarding this resource for our future generations.
There is no quick fix or simple solution when it comes to safeguarding and managing a resource that is in as much demand as water. It is evident that to maintain our most precious resource, our managerial capacity has to be upgraded since the current strategies were developed for past human and natural needs and are now inappropriate to our present needs.

Adapted from Global Environment Outlook for Youth in the Caribbean – A Vision for Change! p. 69

    Technique 2: Determining the Writer's Intention

    This page will include lessons and practice for understanding the writer's purpose in summary writing.

    🧠 What Students Need to Know: Writer’s Intention (5 Key Points)

    Purpose Awareness: Understand why the writer created the text (to inform, persuade, entertain, criticize, explain, etc.).

    Tone and Language: Analyze the mood and word choice: serious, humorous, sarcastic, emotional?

    Main Focus: Identify the central message or argument the writer is trying to convey.

    Audience Clues: Consider who the intended reader is: children, adults, students, professionals?

    Supporting Details: Recognize which ideas support the intention and which are extra or off-topic.

    📘 Example Passage and Step-by-Step Breakdown

    Passage:

    “Many students believe homework is a waste of time. After spending all day in school, they should be allowed to rest or spend time with family. Homework often causes stress and sleep loss. Schools should reconsider how much homework they assign.”

    Step-by-Step to Determine the Writer’s Intention:




    Read Carefully: Understand what the text says literally.

    Ask: What is the main message?
    → That homework is harmful and should be reconsidered.

    Look at tone and evidence:
    → Uses emotional appeals (stress, sleep loss) and speaks strongly against homework.

    Who is being addressed?
    → Likely teachers, school leaders, and possibly parents.

    Conclusion:
    → The writer's intention is to persuade the reader that homework is harmful and should be reduced.

    Technique 3: Removing Repetition

    🧠 What Students Need to Know About Removing Repetition in Summary Writing

    ✅ Why It’s Important:

    • Summaries should be short and clear. Repetition takes up space and adds no new meaning.
    • Repeating the same idea with different words can confuse the reader and weaken the main point.
    • Removing repetition shows that you understand the core message and can express it efficiently.

    ✍️ Tips for Removing Repetition:

    1. Identify the main idea first.
      → Once you know the key message, you can spot when it’s being said again in different ways.
    2. Look for repeated ideas, not just repeated words.
      → Example: “Exercise is good for health” and “Working out helps you stay healthy” mean the same thing.
    3. Choose the clearest sentence and cut the rest.
      → Keep the version that is shortest, simplest, and most directly supports the main idea.
    4. Don’t repeat supporting details either.
      → If two facts or examples prove the same point, keep only one strong one.
    5. Trust your reader to understand the point once.
      → Good summarizers give each idea once, clearly, and move on.

    Technique 4 : Removing Examples

    Why it matters: Examples help explain or support a point in the original passage, but they are often too detailed for a summary. Summaries should keep the idea, not the illustration.

    What students need to know:

    • Examples (like personal stories, case studies, or specific situations) are there to explain a point—not to be repeated in a summary.
    • If a sentence starts with “for example,” “such as,” or “one case was,” it's likely safe to remove.
    • Focus on the general idea the example supports, and state that instead.
    • If the example helps identify the main idea, keep only the main idea—not the details.

    Technique 5: Removing Statistics

    Why it matters: In a summary, your goal is to capture the central message, not the detailed numbers. Most statistics support the main point but are too specific to include.

    What students need to know:

    • Statistics like percentages, years, quantities, and figures are usually supporting evidence—not the main idea.
    • Unless the statistic is essential to understanding the point, leave it out of your summary.
    • Focus on the overall trend or conclusion, not the numbers.

    Technique 6: Using Concise Language

    Why it matters: A summary should be clear, brief, and direct. Long or wordy sentences slow down the reader and add unnecessary detail.

    What students need to know:

    • Use fewer words to say the same thing. Choose strong, precise vocabulary.
    • Avoid repeating ideas or using fillers like “it is important to note that...”
    • Combine or rephrase long sentences to make them shorter without losing meaning.

    Technique 7: Combining Main Ideas to Form a Summary

    Why it matters: A good summary isn't just a list—it flows. You must connect key points to show the overall message of the passage.

    What students need to know:

    • After identifying main ideas in each paragraph, group similar ones together.
    • Use transition words (like "also," "however," or "as a result") to link ideas logically.
    • Your final summary should feel like one clear explanation, not just separate facts.
